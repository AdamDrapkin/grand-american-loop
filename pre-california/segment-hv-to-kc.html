US 66 The Mother Road · Centennial Year 2026
Route 66 was officially named on November 11, 1926 — in Springfield, Missouri. You'll pass through on its 100th birthday year. From St. Louis to Springfield, you're driving the original corridor: the road that carried families, Dust Bowl refugees, and dreamers west for six decades. Ted Drewes is literally on it. Meramec Caverns advertised on barn roofs all the way from Chicago. And Springfield named the whole thing.

Fort Pitt Tunnel → Mount Washington Overlook
Pittsburgh, PA The Reveal Free 45–60 min total

Drive I-376 through the Fort Pitt Tunnel — you're in a mountain, and then in a split second the entire Pittsburgh skyline explodes into view across the Monongahela River. It's one of the most famous tunnel reveals in American driving. Don't look at your phone. Watch the windshield.

Cross to Mount Washington on the south bank for the panoramic overlook view. Three rivers converge directly below. Take the Duquesne Incline (1877 original wooden funicular, $5 round trip — cash/card) 400 feet up for the elevated angle. Small museum at the top covers Pittsburgh's industrial funicular history.

📍 Grandview Overlook 📍 Duquesne Incline
Traffic: Evening rush (4–6pm weekdays) hits Fort Pitt Bridge and Squirrel Hill Tunnel hard. Pass through before 3pm or after 6:30pm. Incline has a 10-min wait on weekend afternoons — before 11am or after 7pm is line-free.
Overnight — Dayton, OH
  • Pure transit overnight. Wright Brothers museum (Dayton Aviation Heritage NHP) is a 30-min detour if arriving before 4pm — free NPS site, genuinely excellent.
  • Any I-70 corridor hotel. Tomorrow's drive is easy — Indy lunch, then St. Louis.

Gateway Arch National Park — St. Louis
Gateway Arch, St. Louis MO Major Stop Museum Free Tram: $26–30 · Book ahead 9am–6pm Sun–Thu · 9am–8pm Fri–Sat

630 feet of stainless steel above the Mississippi. The underground museum covers westward expansion, the Corps of Discovery, and the Arch's construction — it's genuinely excellent and FREE. The tram to the top ($26–30/person) takes you up in 5-person pod capsules.

The Eads Bridge (1874) is a 2-minute walk north — this is where Route 66 travelers originally crossed the Mississippi. Historic and worth seeing from the walkway.

📍 Gateway Arch 📍 Eads Bridge
Summer weekends sell out for the tram. Book at archpark.org before June 16. Museum is always free — tram is the variable. The Arch grounds and Mississippi cobblestone levee are accessible even without a ticket.
Chain of Rocks Bridge — Original Route 66 Mississippi Crossing
10820 Riverview Dr, St. Louis, MO 63137 FREE Pedestrian & bike since 1999 5,353 ft long · Opened 1929

Built in 1929 as one of Route 66's original Mississippi crossings, the Chain of Rocks Bridge has a unique 22-degree bend in the middle — engineered to avoid a rock formation in the river. It was decommissioned in 1970, left for years, and reopened in 1999 as a pedestrian and bike path. Walk the full length: you're literally on the original Route 66, over the Mississippi, on a bridge that's been unchanged since 1929.

The views are remarkable — the river is wide here, with the St. Louis skyline visible to the south. Historic water intake towers mid-river add to the atmosphere. On a clear afternoon, you can see for miles.

📍 Open in Google Maps
Located at the north edge of St. Louis — about 15 min from the Arch. Free parking lot on the Missouri side. Walk to the bend, take photos, walk back. 45 min total. The Illinois side has a trailhead for the Ted Jones Trail if you want to extend further.
City Museum — St. Louis
750 N 16th St, St. Louis, MO 63103 $34.99 Summer Pass · 10am–6pm 314-231-2489 All ages · unforgettable

A former shoe warehouse turned into one of the most unusual buildings in America. Part museum, part giant indoor/outdoor playground, part art installation. Caves, tunnels, slides (including a 10-story spiral slide), a rooftop Ferris wheel, an aquarium, circus arts, and architecture salvaged from demolished St. Louis buildings — all incorporated into the structure itself. There is nothing else like it anywhere.

The Summer Play Pass ($34.99) includes rooftop access. Bring layers — the basement caves are cold. There are no "rules" — you crawl, climb, slide, and explore at your own pace. Adults love it as much as kids.

📍 Open in Google Maps
City Museum gets very busy on summer weekends — if you're arriving on a Saturday or Sunday, go right when it opens at 10am. The caves and underground sections are the most interesting and least crowded during peak hours. City Nights events (21+) run Thursday–Saturday evenings if you want an adults-only version.

  • Hours: Summer 11am–midnight (roughly). Window service only. Cash or card. No seating.

🛣️ The Actual Route 66 — Where It Goes From Here
From St. Louis westward, Route 66 runs almost exactly parallel to I-44. When you're on I-44, you're on the Route 66 corridor. The old highway exists as frontage roads alongside the interstate in many sections — you can dip off and drive the original pavement. Key: the route heads through Watson Road (southwest St. Louis), then into the Ozark plateau, following the Meramec River valley west.
  • St. Louis (Watson Rd / Chippewa St) — The original Route 66 westbound alignment leaving the city
  • Route 66 State Park, Eureka (~30 min from STL) — 409 acres on the former Times Beach EPA Superfund site, now a park with a Route 66 museum pavilion. Quick stop, free to walk.
  • Meramec Caverns, Stanton — Jesse James hideout, advertised on barn rooftops across the Midwest since the 1930s. ON the route.
  • Cuba, MO — "Route 66 Mural City" — 12 officially commissioned murals depicting local history. Right on I-44/Route 66.
  • Lebanon, MO — The Munger Moss Motel (1946, original neon sign still lit) is the best-preserved Route 66 motel in Missouri.
  • Springfield, MO — Where Route 66 was officially named on November 11, 1926. Centennial year 2026.
Johnson's Shut-Ins State Park — SWIM STOP
Hwy 21 detour south of I-44 · Lesterville, MO Swim Stop $7/vehicle 2–3 hrs · Green flag = open

The East Fork Black River carved through 1.5-billion-year-old Precambrian rhyolite into a natural water park of chutes, channels, slides, and ice-cold deep pools. Nothing manmade. The rock is smooth from a billion years of water. The water is cold from Ozark springs. In June with high humidity — this is the best possible break from the interstate.

Check mostateparks.com morning of June 17 for flag status. Green = open. Arrive by 9am to park close.

📍 Open in Google Maps
~1.5 hrs south of I-44 from St. Louis via Hwy 21. Weekend crowds are heavy — arrive by 9am. The parking lot fills by mid-morning in summer.
Meramec Caverns — Jesse James Hideout
I-44 Exit 230, Stanton, MO $30.50/adult Guided 80-min tour Tours every 20–30 min from 9am

Five-level limestone cave system. A constant 60°F inside year-round. Tours every 20–30 minutes. Meramec Caverns has been a Route 66 landmark since the 1930s — the owner hired people to paint the cavern's name on barn roofs across a 12-state area. You've seen those barns your whole life. This is the cave. Jesse James allegedly used it as a hideout after bank robberies. Bring a light layer for the 60°F interior.

📍 Open in Google Maps
Cuba, MO — "Route 66 Mural City"
Cuba, MO · I-44 Exit 208 Free 12 official murals · 20-min stop Wagon Wheel Motel · 80+ years

Cuba commissioned 12 large-scale murals through the Viva Cuba Murals Project — painted on building walls throughout town, each depicting scenes from local and Route 66 history. Pick up a walking map at the Cuba Visitor Center and do the loop. 20 minutes, completely free, genuinely impressive.

The Wagon Wheel Motel on Route 66 in Cuba is the longest continuously-operating motel on Route 66 — over 80 years in the same spot. The original neon sign, the original cabins. You can look; you can also stay if you want a real Route 66 night.

Missouri Hick BBQ (Cuba) — Old West décor, ribs/brisket/pulled pork. Local favorite. Good lunch or early dinner option.

📍 Cuba Mural City 📍 Wagon Wheel Motel
Springfield, MO — Where Route 66 Was Born
Springfield, MO · I-44 terminus city Named Here Nov 11, 1926 Overnight

Route 66 was officially named at the Colonial Hotel in Springfield, Missouri on November 11, 1926. Its centennial is November 2026. Tonight you're sleeping in the city where it all started. The Route 66 Car Museum (1634 W Route 66 Blvd, check hours) has 80+ vehicles from the Route 66 era — worth a morning stop.

Lambert's Café (Ozark, 10 min south) — the only restaurant in America where dinner rolls are thrown at you from across the room. 4.6⭐ / 27,972 reviews. Arrive at 5pm or after 8pm for shortest wait.

Joe's Kansas City Bar-B-Que
3002 W 47th Ave, Kansas City, KS (in a gas station) Top 5 BBQ in America 4.8⭐ · 17,469 reviews Mon–Sat 11am–9pm (10pm Fri–Sat)

Operates inside a working Quik Trip gas station and has for decades. Ranked consistently top-5 BBQ in America. Order the Z-Man: slow-smoked brisket on a kaiser roll with smoked provolone and crispy onion rings. $15. It will ruin you for other sandwiches.

Kearney, NE — The Great Plains Begin
  • After Kansas City, I-80 west opens into enormous sky, grain elevators on the horizon, and the Platte River valley. You're on the Oregon/California/Mormon Trail corridor.
  • Kearney is 2,150 ft elevation — evenings cool to the upper 50s vs KC's heat. Kearney Archway Monument straddles I-80 at Exit 272 — large museum on the Great Plains migration trails. $14/adult, open ~10am–7pm.
  • Tomorrow is the hardest drive day of the entire trip (571 mi). Sleep early.
